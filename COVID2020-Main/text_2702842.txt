SINGAPORE, March 2 (Reuters) - The coronavirus epidemic has spread from China to more than 30 countries, heightening pandemic fears and worries about global recession and sparking a flight by foreign funds out of Asia's emerging markets from currencies to stocks and bonds. Here is the latest snapshot of outflows and measures in response: INDONESIA Non-resident investors sold 16.43 trillion rupiah ($1.2 billion) of tradable government bonds over the first three days of last week, according to the latest government data. Indonesia's rupiah has fallen more than 5% in two weeks. The yield on 10-year Indonesian sovereign bonds has jumped 50 basis points in four trading sessions . The central bank on Monday said it would cut banks' reserve requirements for savings in foreign currencies by 400 basis points to 4%, freeing $3.2 billion, and increase its own intervention in markets to hold the currency steady. SOUTH KOREA Last week, foreign investors sold a net 3.6 trillion won ($3 billion) worth of stocks listed on the KOSPI and KOSDAQ indexes, the biggest outflow since the 2008 financial crisis. The won has dropped 3.3% against the dollar since January. The vice finance minister has vowed to use "all possible measures" to stabilise markets, though the central bank held interest rates steady last week and stressed the limits of monetary policy. MALAYSIA As domestic political turmoil also hurt sentiment, foreign investors dumped 1.26 billion ringgit ($300 million) worth of Malaysian equities last week, the largest weekly outflow in 20 months. For February, foreigners liquidated nearly 2 billion ringgit in stocks, the highest such outflow since August. The currency has lost 3.4% against the dollar since Jan. 20. TAIWAN Foreign investors sold T$94.4 billion ($3.14 billion) in Taiwanese stocks last week, the third worst weekly performance in history. The benchmark index shed 3.4% last week. THAILAND Foreigners liquidated 17.9 billion baht ($572 million) in bonds last week, the biggest selldown in six months. It was also the seventh straight week of foreigners selling stocks, with a net 4.2 billion baht outflow. Thailand's currency has been among the worst performing in Asia this year, dropping almost 5% against the dollar.
